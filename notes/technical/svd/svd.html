Intuition

A geometric fact:

The image of the unit sphere under any matrix A∈Rm×nA \in \mathbb{R^{m \times n}}A∈Rm×n﻿ is a hyperellipse.

What’s a hyperellipse?

PCA

Given points x1,…xn∈Rmx_1, \dots x_n \in \mathbb{R^m}x1​,…xn​∈Rm﻿

Goal: find a kkk﻿-dimensional subspace SSS﻿ (where k<<mk << mk<<m﻿) that is close to these points.

Def. Ps:=P_s :=Ps​:=﻿ orthogonal projection onto SSS﻿.

Solution: S={u1,…,uk}S = \{u_1, \dots, u_k \}S={u1​,…,uk​}﻿, the left singular vectors of X∈Rm×nX \in \mathbb{R^{m \times n}}X∈Rm×n﻿.

Moreover, the approximation error is the sum of squares of the “neglected” singular values:

PCA Algorithm

  1. Center the data (subtract mean).

    Empirical mean: μ=1n∑i=1nxi∈Rm\mu = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n x_i \in \mathbb{R^m}μ=n1​∑i=1n​xi​∈Rm﻿

      ⟹  xˉi=xi=μ\implies \bar{x}_i = x_i = \mu⟹xˉi​=xi​=μ﻿

  1. Compute SVD of [xˉ1∣…∣xˉn]\left[\bar{x}_1 | \dots | \bar{x}_n\right][xˉ1​∣…∣xˉn​]﻿.

      ⟹  UˉΣˉVˉT\implies \bar{U}\bar{\Sigma}\bar{V}^T⟹UˉΣˉVˉT﻿

  1. Get the kkk﻿-dimensional PCA projection: UˉkTxˉi∈Rk\bar{U}_k^T \bar{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^kUˉkT​xˉi​∈Rk﻿.

Based on Eckart-Young-Mirsky theorem, this is the best low-rank subspace!

Intuition

PCA can be interpreted as fitting a kkk﻿-dimensional hyperellipsoid to the data.

  • Each axis of the ellipsoid represents a principal component.

Why do we need to center the data?

  • This is equivalent to moving the hyperellipsoid to the origin.
  • If we don’t, the first singular vector will influence the mean:
    • u1≈μu_1 \approx \muu1​≈μ﻿. Why? because μ\muμ﻿ minimizes 1n∑i=1n∣∣xi−μ∣∣2\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n ||x_i - \mu||^2n1​∑i=1n​∣∣xi​−μ∣∣2﻿
  • We want to choose vectors orthogonal to zero, not restricted to be orthogonal to u1u_1u1​﻿.

Computing the SVD

How do we actually compute the (full) SVD?

Idea: turn it into an eigenvalue decomposition problem (power iterations!).

Iteratively compute eigenpairs of ATAA^TAATA﻿ to get UUU﻿.

Iteratively compute eigenpairs of AATAA^TAAT﻿ to get VVV﻿.

Take square root of eigenvalues from above to get Σ\SigmaΣ﻿.

Drawbacks of the SVD

Interpretability problem:

  • Singular vector is a linear combination of all input columns/rows
  • How do we interpret that?

Lack of sparsity:

  • Singular vectors are dense!
